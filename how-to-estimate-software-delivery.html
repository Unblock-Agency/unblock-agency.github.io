Why Estimate Hours vs Story Points When & How Capacity Planning Mistakes to Avoid Wrap-Up

How to Estimate Software Delivery

Estimating software delivery timelines is a critical part of managing a startup engineering team. Done right, it gives founders the confidence to plan and execute without constantly putting out fires. This guide walks you through how to do it accurately—using our Capacity Planning Sheet as your foundation.

Why Estimation Matters

For early-stage startups, misestimating timelines can lead to delayed launches, missed opportunities, and overwhelmed teams. Accurate forecasting helps you stay lean and organized while building quality products quickly. Our method ensures your team is focused, accountable, and delivering on what matters.

When to Estimate

Estimation occurs during Sprint Planning. Ideally, before the planning meeting, product owners and leads prepare a prioritized task list. During the meeting, each ticket is reviewed, discussed, and estimated. These estimates are then compared to the team’s available capacity for the sprint.

Choosing Your Estimation Unit

  • Junior Teams: Use hours for clarity and specificity.
  • Senior Teams: Use story points for flexibility and abstraction.

In either case, consistency and understanding across the team are key.

How to Use the Capacity Planning Sheet

1. Sprint Configuration

This section defines the overall sprint parameters and working context. Fill out the following fields:

  • Number of Days in Sprint: Usually 10 (for two-week sprints).
  • Working Day (in hours): Typically 8 hours.
  • Company Holidays: Days off due to holidays.
  • Lost Work Days: Sprint delays or early closures.
  • Time for Sprint Ceremonies: Est. hours per person (e.g., 2-3 hrs).
  • Time for Daily Standups: Usually 0.25 hrs x number of days.
  • Non-Admin %: Time available for actual development (default 80%).

2. Team PTO

List each team member and how many full days they are taking off during the sprint. This directly impacts their availability and is factored into the next section.

3. Team Capacity Configuration

This calculates each team member’s total available hours:

  • PTO Hours: Days off multiplied by daily working hours.
  • Ceremony/Standup Time: From the configuration above.
  • Non-Admin Time: 80% of working hours minus ceremonies and PTO.
  • Availability: Final number of hours available to write code or design features.

The sheet is color-coded by department (e.g., design, engineering, QA) and each member has a column. This gives you a full snapshot of team-wide capacity.

4. Tickets in Sprint and Estimates

During Sprint Planning:

  • Review prioritized tasks from your task manager (e.g., Linear, Jira).
  • Assign each task to a specific team member.
  • Write the estimate (hours or story points) in the cell matching the team member and ticket.

By the end of the meeting, every member has clearly defined work with time estimates that match their available hours.

5. Team Sprint Allocation / Utilization

This last section summarizes how well you’ve allocated your team:

  • Total Hours Allocated: Sum of assigned estimates per person.
  • Total Hours Remaining: Difference between capacity and allocation.
  • Sprint Utilization: Percentage of team capacity filled.

If a developer is at 100%, they’re fully booked. If someone is below 80%, consider redistributing work. If over 100%, they’re likely to burn out or miss deadlines.

Final Notes

The Capacity Planning Sheet doesn’t just help you plan—it helps you communicate. It becomes a source of truth during reviews, and a planning tool for founders who want to stay focused on outcomes, not the chaos of task-level execution.

Download the sheet, customize it for your team, and use it as part of every sprint cycle. It will transform how you approach delivery, team health, and business timelines.
